A cuneiform inscription carved high on a cliff at Bisitun overlooking an important route between Mesopotamia and Iran.


A drawing of part of the relief at Bisitun.

It was carved on the orders of the Persian king Darius I who is pictured above the inscription.

It shows the king resting a foot on one of his enemies with rebels tied together in front of him.

The cuneiform gives the same information about how Darius had defeated his enemies in three different languages: Old Persian, Babylonian and Elamite.

Rawlinson worked out which cuneiform signs spelt the name Darius in Persian and compared them with the signs used to write the name in Babylonian. He then deciphered many of the other signs and words.
